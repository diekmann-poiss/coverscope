📚 Methodology

Overview

CoverScope measures living green vegetation coverage from a photo, entirely on device. Deterministic and reproducible — no internet required for basic analysis, with enhanced research-backed algorithms for improved accuracy.

Scientific Foundation

Our plant detection methodology is based on peer-reviewed research in remote sensing, precision agriculture, and computer vision. All vegetation indices and detection methods implemented have been validated in scientific literature.

Vegetation Indices

1. Excess-Green Index (ExG)

Formula: ExG = (2 × Green) − Red − Blue

Source: Woebbecke et al. (1995) "Verification of color vegetation indices for automated crop imaging applications" (Computers and Electronics in Agriculture)

Purpose: Primary vegetation detection index. ExG amplifies greenness by leveraging color-component differences, providing a near-binary intensity image that clearly outlines plant regions. Highly effective for distinguishing vegetation from soil and background.

2. Color Index of Vegetation Extraction (CIVE)

Formula: CIVE = (Green − Red) / (Green + Red + Blue)

Source: Kataoka et al. (2003) "A universal estimation model of fractional vegetation cover" (Remote Sensing of Environment)

Purpose: Enhances crop vs. background discrimination. Particularly effective for fractional vegetation cover (FVC) estimation across different crop types. Note: We use the original CIVE formula without the +0.5 offset for better sensitivity.

3. Green-Red Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (GRNDVI)

Formula: GRNDVI = (Green − Red) / (Green + Red)

Source: Adapted from NDVI methodology. Note: True NDVI uses Near-Infrared (NIR) band: NDVItrue = (NIR − Red) / (NIR + Red). GRNDVI is the RGB-adapted version.

Purpose: Normalized difference index adapted for RGB cameras, measuring green-red contrast.

4. Visible Atmospherically Resistant Index (VARI)

Formula: VARI = (Green − Red) / (Green + Red − Blue)

Source: Gitelson, A. A., et al. (2002) "Remote estimation of canopy chlorophyll content in higher plants" (Geophysical Research Letters)

Purpose: Atmospherically resistant vegetation index designed for RGB imagery. More stable under varying illumination conditions.

5. Excess Green minus Excess Red (ExGR)

Formula: ExGR = (2×Green − Red − Blue) − (1.4×Red − Green)

Source: Mexson, J. D., & Chmielewski, T. A. (2010) "Vegetation detection using vegetation indices algorithm supported by statistical machine learning" (Environmental Monitoring and Assessment)

Purpose: Combines ExG with excess red suppression for improved plant/background separation.

6. Enhanced ExG

Formula: Enhanced = ExG × (Green / (Red + Blue + 1))

Source: Custom enhancement combining ExG with green ratio for improved sensitivity.

Purpose: Weighted ExG that accounts for relative green intensity, improving detection in mixed lighting conditions.

HSV Color Space Detection

Hue Range: 40°–180° (captures green to yellow-green vegetation)

Saturation: > 0.1 and < 0.9 (filters out grays/near-whites and oversaturated artificial colors)

Value: > 0.08 (filters out very dark shadows)

Source: "Greenness identification based on HSV decision tree" (2014). Research validates hue ranges of 50°–150° and 70°–180° for healthy green vegetation. Our wider range (40°–180°) captures additional plant types and lighting variations. The saturation upper bound (s < 0.9) prevents false positives from oversaturated non-vegetation colors.

Multi-Method Ensemble Approach

Instead of relying on a single index, CoverScope uses a voting system with 9 different detection methods. A pixel is classified as plant if at least 1 method confirms it (configurable via sensitivity slider).

Research Support: "State of Major Vegetation Indices in Precision Agriculture Studies" (Agriculture, 2023) shows that "integrating these indices—ExG, CIVE, NDVI, and others—into ensemble models can improve the robustness and accuracy of plant detection."

Included Methods (9 total):

  1. HSV-based green detection (Hue 40°–180°, Saturation 0.1–0.9, Value > 0.08)
  2. Enhanced ExG with green dominance
  3. ExG with relaxed threshold (ExG > effectiveThreshold × 0.5)
  4. Normalized ExG (2G − R − B > 15)
  5. CIVE (CIVE > 0.1 with sufficient green)
  6. GRNDVI (> 0.1 with green dominance) - RGB-adapted NDVI
  7. VARI (> 0.1 with green dominance and g > 70) - Atmospherically resistant
  8. ExGR (ExGR > 0 with green dominance) - Excess Green minus Excess Red
  9. Bright Green (Green > 100, Red/Blue < 0.9 × Green)

Green-Dominance Check

In addition to color indices, we verify: Green > Red AND Green > Blue

Source: Standard practice in RGB vegetation detection to ensure green channel dominance, as healthy vegetation reflects more green light than red or blue.

Adaptive Thresholding

The sensitivity slider adjusts the detection threshold dynamically based on image brightness and contrast. A brightness factor modifies the base threshold to maintain accuracy across different lighting conditions.

Sensitivity Settings

Aggressive: Lower threshold — catches faint green, more inclusive (higher recall, potential false positives)

Moderate: Balanced detection (default)

Conservative: Higher threshold — only strong green, more exclusive (higher precision, potential false negatives)

Scale Calibration

  1. Select two points of known real-world distance
  2. Enter the actual distance between them (cm or m)
  3. App calculates: metresPerPixel = realDistance / pixelDistance
  4. Plant area = plantPixels × (metresPerPixel)²

Note: Only vertical or horizontal calibration lines are accepted for accuracy.

Health Index

Formula: Health = (Average Saturation + Average Green Value) / 2

Computes average color saturation and green channel intensity across detected plant pixels, providing a relative health metric (0-100%).

Manual Correction

  • Add Mode: Paint missed plants (false negatives)
  • Erase Mode: Remove false positives

Target Area

Focus analysis on a specific region rather than the entire image. Useful for analyzing specific plants or excluding background areas.

1m² Reference Square

Drag the 1m² reference square anywhere on your calibrated image for instant coverage percentage of that patch. Useful for consistent measurements across multiple images.

Cubic Volume Estimation

  1. Place 1m² square on target area
  2. Enter plant heights at 4 corners + center
  3. Volume (m³) = Average Height × Coverage % × 1m²
